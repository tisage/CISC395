Tokyo, Japan — Travel Guide

Tokyo is one of the most organized and efficient cities in the world. The city runs on an extensive subway network that connects all major districts. Trains are almost always on time, and station staff are helpful even if they don't speak English. Most signs in major stations have English translations.

Best Season: Late March to early April for cherry blossoms (sakura), or late October to mid-November for fall foliage. Summer (July–August) is hot and humid. Golden Week (late April to early May) is very crowded — book accommodation months in advance.

Food: Tokyo has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other city. Must-try foods include fresh sushi and sashimi at Tsukiji Outer Market (the inner market moved to Toyosu, but the outer market stalls remain open), tonkotsu ramen in Shinjuku's ramen alleys, and yakitori skewers near Yurakucho station under the train tracks. Convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) sell surprisingly good onigiri and hot meals at low prices.

Activities: Visit Senso-ji temple in Asakusa early morning before crowds arrive. Explore Shibuya Crossing at night. Day trip to Nikko (2 hours by train) for shrines and waterfalls. Akihabara for electronics and anime merchandise. Harajuku's Takeshita Street for street fashion.

Practical Tips: Get a Suica IC card at any major station — it works on trains, buses, and even convenience stores. Most restaurants display plastic food models or picture menus outside. Tipping is not customary in Japan and can be considered rude.

Budget: Mid-range traveler should expect 8,000–15,000 yen per day (roughly $55–$100 USD) including food, transport, and accommodation in a business hotel.
